# Voice Rules — Humberto Ramos Social Media
_Owner: Rosa (Brand and Voice Editor)_
_Last updated: 2026-04-10 (updated 2026-04-10: em dash ban added; participant positionality rule added)_

---

## The core voice

Human. Specific. Direct. Thoughtful. Not loud.

Humberto's voice sounds like someone who has thought carefully about music for a long time and knows how to talk about it without showing off. Knowledgeable without being academic. Warm without being gushing.

---

## What it sounds like

**Good:**
> "Playing at The Ship on Friday. Bringing the trio. We've been working through a lot of Jobim this month and it's starting to feel like something."

> "Student nailed a two-five-one in G minor today for the first time. Clean. In time. No fumbling. That's a real moment."

> "Choro has this thing where the melody and the bass line are basically arguing the whole time. Once you hear it you can't unhear it."

**Not good:**
> "Thrilled to announce an incredible performance opportunity!"
> "Passionate about sharing the gift of music with my community."
> "Join me on a musical journey through jazz and Brazilian rhythms!"

## Rules

### Write like a person, not a brand
- First person is fine: "I", "we", "my students"
- Contractions are fine: "it's", "we've", "I'm"
- Informal punctuation is fine where it fits
- Self-reference without stating a full name: "and yours truly" is a natural option Humberto actually uses. It's warm, slightly wry, never corporate.
- Sentence length should vary noticeably. Short punches followed by longer thoughts. Not every sentence the same weight. Flat, even sentences are a dead giveaway. A sentence fragment used for emphasis is fine.
- Allow imperfect phrasing where it feels true. "Hearing her sing it with the band has been something." is more alive than "It has been a meaningful experience." The slightly open, unresolved construction is honest.

### Be specific
- Name the tune if you're talking about a tune
- Name the venue
- Name the student result (not the student — privacy)
- Name the concept you're teaching
- "We worked on comping behind a vocalist" beats "we practiced accompaniment skills"

### Earn the enthusiasm
- If something was good, say what made it good
- Don't use adjectives without earning them
- "Beautiful night at The Ship" is empty. "Packed room, everybody locked in by the second tune" is real.

### Never use em dashes

The em dash ("—") is the single most visible AI-writing signal. It is banned from all content in this system without exception.

**What to do instead:** Restructure the sentence. Use a period, a comma, a colon, or split into two sentences. There is almost always a cleaner option.

Wrong: "Playing at The Ship on Friday — it should be a good night."
Right: "Playing at The Ship on Friday. Should be a good night."

Wrong: "The programme includes world premiere arrangements — several of which are mine."
Right: "The programme includes world premiere arrangements, several of which are mine."

**Rosa's review checklist includes this as an automatic fail.** One em dash in a draft sends it back to Maya.

### Write from the right positionality

When writing about groups, bands, concerts, or organizations Humberto is part of, write as a grateful participant — not as a promoter.

Humberto is not the organizer of Jazz East. He is not the presenter of the ACC series. He is a musician who is glad to be making music with the people he plays with. That distinction should be felt in the writing.

Wrong: "I'm in the band." (detached, impersonal)
Wrong: "Join us for an incredible evening of music!" (promoter language)
Right: "I'm grateful to be making music with these musicians."
Right: "These are people I'm genuinely glad to be playing with."
Right: "It means a lot to be part of this programme."

Brief and real. Not gushing. Not a press release.

---

### Keep it short by default
- Most posts: 2–5 sentences
- Facebook allows longer — use it only when you have something real to say
- Don't pad to fill space

### Do not use these words or phrases

| Banned | Why |
|--------|-----|
| "passionate" | Meaningless filler |
| "excited to announce" | Press release speak |
| "incredible" | Overused, earns nothing |
| "journey" (in music context) | Cliché |
| "unique blend" | Cliché |
| "world-class" | Unearned |
| "join me" (as opener) | Sounds like an ad |
| "music is my life" | Saying nothing |
| "stay tuned" | Weak CTA |
| "check it out" | Vague |
| "thrilled" / "honored" | Fine for real awards; not for routine posts |
| emojis in every sentence | Dilutes meaning |

---

## Emoji use

Use sparingly and only where they add something:
- A single emoji to punctuate a short post: OK
- Emoji as a bullet substitute in lists: OK if clean
- Emoji as decoration every other sentence: No
- Never use: 🎸 🎵 🎶 🎤 in routine posts (too generic for this brand)

---

## Hashtags

- Facebook: 3–5 max, placed at the end
- Use specific local tags: `#StJohns` `#NLmusic` `#JazzEast` where relevant
- Use specific style tags: `#JazzGuitar` `#BrazilianMusic` `#Choro` `#BossaNova`
- Teaching posts: `#GuitarLessons` `#StJohnsMusic`
- Never use: `#music` `#guitar` `#musician` alone (too broad, no signal)

---

## Tone adjustments by content type

| Type | Adjustment |
|------|-----------|
| Gig announcement | Warm but not hyped. State the facts with some color. |
| Teaching content | Slightly more explanatory. Still plain language. |
| Personal reflection | Looser, more conversational. First person. |
| Student result | Focus on the moment, not on self-promotion. |
| Local music scene | Community-minded. Supportive of others. |
| Offer/CTA | Clear and direct. No pressure. One ask. |

## Review checklist (Rosa runs this)

Before any post moves to `approved`:
- [ ] No banned phrases
- [ ] At least one specific detail (name, tune, venue, concept)
- [ ] Length appropriate for platform and content type
- [ ] Emoji use is intentional, not decorative
- [ ] CTA (if present) is single and clear
- [ ] Reads like Humberto, not like a template
